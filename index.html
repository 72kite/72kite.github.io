    A prefix tree walks one node per character you type. A linear scan checks every item, every keystroke. Grow the inventory to 10,000 items and the right-hand number becomes 10,000 — the left-hand number stays exactly where it is. That property is the reason I replaced the search in my capstone project.

    Professional self-assessment

    I build software across the stack, and the work I care most about is the part users never see: the decision underneath the screen. My capstone took an Android inventory app I wrote earlier in the program and rebuilt it three times over — once for structure, once for speed, once for safety.

    What that process taught me is that the three are the same skill. A Repository sitting between the ViewModel and the data layer is what made swapping raw SQLite for an encrypted Room database a contained change rather than a rewrite. Good structure is what buys you the freedom to fix things later.

    I am looking for software engineering work where security is treated as a design constraint from the first commit rather than a task filed for after launch.

    Algorithms & data structures

    Integration pending

    Search was a full-table scan on every keystroke. I designed a Trie index that resolves a prefix in steps proportional to the length of the query instead of the size of the inventory, plus a MergeSort pass for stable ordering of results. The demo at the top of this page runs the same structure in the browser. Integration into the Repository is the next step.

    Databases

    Complete

    The app stored inventory in an unencrypted SQLite file — readable by anyone who pulled it off the device. I migrated it to Room backed by SQLCipher with 256-bit AES, and moved the passphrase out of the source and into runtime. The proof is in the first sixteen bytes of the database file:

    before 53 51 4C 69 74 65 20 66 6F 72 6D 61 74 20 33 00 → "SQLite format 3"
    after A7 3C 1E 90 44 D2 0B 5F E1 88 7A 33 C6 09 2D B4 → ciphertext
